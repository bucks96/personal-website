## Search revamp:

This was about three to four months into my time at Supertails. We were a pet-care e-commerce platform with over 20,000 SKUs, and search was one of the primary ways users discovered products. On the surface, the numbers looked fine — search was getting good traffic and decent conversions. But when I actually went in and used the search myself, I found serious gaps. Generic queries like "dog food" worked okay, but the moment you searched something specific — say "5 kg dog food for Labrador puppy" — the results were either partial matches, wrong products, or worse, zero results. And these long-tail searches are often the highest-intent users — people who know exactly what they want and are ready to buy.
I first evaluated whether we should fix this in-house or bring in a specialized tool. Given the complexity of search — handling synonyms, ranking, learning from behavior — I made the call that a third-party tool was the right move. I ran a structured evaluation across cost, algorithm quality, ability to handle our SKU volume, and one thing that was non-negotiable for me: the ability to manually intervene in search rankings. I landed on a tool called Findify. It was expensive — roughly 10x what we were paying before — so there were multiple rounds of negotiation before we got sign-off.
The integration itself took about two to three months — syncing our product feed in real-time, customizing the UI, ensuring inventory changes reflected instantly.
When we launched, conversions were the same as before. Which was a problem, because we were now paying 10x. But I knew the improvement would come from two levers. First, the algorithm was self-learning — the more people searched and purchased, the smarter it got at ranking. That was a long-term play. Second, and this is where I spent most of my energy — manual intervention. I set up a daily review cadence with the category managers, which we later moved to weekly once things stabilized. Every week, we'd go through individual search terms, look at what was converting and what wasn't, and take corrective action — pinning the right products, adding banners, aligning search results with whatever marketing campaigns were driving traffic that week. It was unglamorous, operationally heavy work, but it compounded.
Over about nine to ten months, we doubled our search conversion rate — from roughly 7% to 14%. And by then, search was driving about 55% of total platform revenue. The ₹60 crore ARR impact made it one of the highest-ROI initiatives during my time there.


## Finding new observations in data:

This was at Supertails. The marketing team ran campaigns across Google and Meta, and one of the Google campaign types — Performance Max — had always been flagged as low-quality traffic. The conversion rates were poor, and we'd been told from the start not to expect much from it. Everyone had accepted that as a given.
I didn't challenge that assumption intentionally — I just happened to dig into the data one day and noticed something interesting. The users coming through PerfMax weren't low-intent. They were searching for specific products — say, "Pedigree chicken flavor 3kg." Google would show them a carousel of sponsored ads, all Pedigree products but different variants. They'd click on one, land on our product page, and immediately see the packaging front and center — only to realize it's the wrong flavor.
And here's the problem: at that point, we gave them no way to course-correct. There was no "other flavors" section, no "more from this brand" — nothing. So they'd hit back, return to Google, and often end up on a competitor's site. We were losing high-intent users not because of bad traffic quality, but because of a bad landing experience.
The fix was straightforward. I added a contextual product recommendation module on the product page — "other products from this brand" or "similar products in this category," depending on the context. It wasn't a massive engineering lift, but it fundamentally changed the exit path for these users. Instead of bouncing back to Google, they now had a reason to stay and browse.
The result: Add-to-Cart rate for this segment increased by about 8%, which translated to roughly ₹20–25 crore in incremental ARR.
