## Hamburger navigation revamp story at Vaaree: 

This happened in literally my first week at Vaaree. I was doing introductory meetings with people across teams — just getting to know everyone and how things worked. During one of these calls with a category manager, they needed to show me something in the All Categories section. So they opened the hamburger menu, and I noticed it took them a couple of seconds to find it. They work here every day and they're struggling to locate the primary navigation item.
That stuck with me. After the meeting, I went and pulled up the data — and the numbers confirmed exactly what I'd observed. Nearly 70% of users were dropping off after opening the hamburger menu without ever reaching All Categories. The thing they came to find was essentially buried.
I took this to the design team, we redesigned the menu layout, and we shipped it within two weeks. The result: the number of users reaching All Categories after opening the hamburger menu doubled — a 100% increase. And because these were high-intent users who were actively trying to browse, conversions through this pathway doubled as well, adding roughly ₹4–5 crore in incremental ARR.


## App download story at Vaaree: 

When I joined Vaaree, the app had been live for about a week. It had under 1,000 users. The cofounders' brief was simple: get the app adopted and bring it to feature parity with the website. I owned both surfaces, so this was squarely on me.
I broke the problem into two phases: first, make the app worth keeping; then, get people to download it. In that order — because pushing acquisition before the product is ready just burns trust.
Phase one was about product readiness. I ran a thorough internal QA pass with the testing team, fixed all the critical bugs, and then did something that turned out to be really valuable — I gave the app to people across the org for beta testing. Category managers, CX team, marketing — people who weren't close to the product. That surfaced a bunch of UX issues we'd missed. We prioritized by effort and impact, shipped the fixes, and pushed a clean release. I also set up a standing feedback loop with the Customer Experience team — they would monitor app reviews and flag any app-specific complaints from their support channels back to us on a weekly basis. That loop stayed active throughout, not just at launch.
Phase two was acquisition — and I wanted to do it organically first before spending anything on paid campaigns. The insight was simple: we already had traffic on the website. So I embedded app download nudges into existing high-intent user flows. A sticky banner at the top offering an app-exclusive discount. A nudge on the order confirmation screen — you've just bought something, you're happy, here's a reason to download the app. These weren't interruptive pop-ups; they were placed at moments where the user was already engaged and the app was a natural next step.
Once we were confident the app was stable and the organic flywheel was working, the marketing team layered on paid app-install campaigns and retention flows for new app users. But by that point, the foundation was solid — people were downloading an app that actually worked well and had feature parity with the website.
The result: we went from under 1,000 MAU to over 80,000 MAU in about nine months — entirely through this phased approach of fix first, then acquire organically, then amplify with paid.
